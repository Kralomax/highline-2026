üèîÔ∏è Trail Overview

Blue Ridge (Passage 27) is the PERFECT family backpacking trip - shorter distance, moderate difficulty, and you start ON TOP of the Mogollon Rim!

‚ú® Why Your Family Will Love This Trail

START ON TOP! Begin at 7,600' on the Mogollon Rim - skip the brutal climb. You start where the Highline Trail finishes!

SHORTEST OPTION: Only 15.4 miles over 2-3 days. Perfect for mixed experience levels and "old legs."

BEST WATER: East Clear Creek is cold, clear, reliable, and perfect for an afternoon fishing session!

FLEXIBLE: Do 2 days (faster) or 3 days (relaxed). Your choice!

MODERATE DIFFICULTY: Only 1,397' total elevation gain. Mostly downhill or flat!

üå°Ô∏è Weather Expectations (August/September)

Late August (Aug 25-31): Highs 84-86¬∞F, Lows 64¬∞F, Higher humidity (~75%)

Early September (Sept 1-7): Highs 79-83¬∞F, Lows 59-60¬∞F, Lower humidity (~78%), Less rain

Pro Tip: Either timeframe works great! September is slightly cooler and drier, but August is still excellent hiking weather in the mountains. Both are MUCH cooler than Arizona lowlands!

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Trail Notes

Shuttle Required: This is a point-to-point trail. You'll need a car at BOTH ends (or arrange a shuttle).

Water: Filter/treat all water from East Clear Creek. It's reliable but not potable without treatment.

Permits: NO permits required! Just show up and hike. (Free parking at both trailheads.)

Cell Service: Spotty to none. Download maps offline!

Wildlife: Black bears and elk present. Hang food properly!

üé£ Afternoon Fishing Session

üí™ Training & Preparation

Get your body ready for 3 days of hiking! Even moderate trails are easier when you're prepared.

‚ú® The Good News!

This is a MODERATE trail! You don't need to be an ultra-marathoner. If you can hike 5-6 miles with a backpack, you're ready!

Low daily mileage: Only 5 miles per day on the 3-day itinerary. Very doable!

Gradual elevation: No brutal climbs. Most of the trail is downhill or flat.

üìÖ 8-Week Training Plan

Weeks Focus Workout
Weeks 1-2 Base Building ‚Ä¢ Hike 2-3 miles, 2-3 times per week (flat terrain, no pack)
‚Ä¢ Add 30-minute walks on rest days
‚Ä¢ Focus on consistency over intensity
Weeks 3-4 Add Distance ‚Ä¢ Hike 3-4 miles, 2-3 times per week
‚Ä¢ Start carrying a light daypack (10-15 lbs)
‚Ä¢ Add some hills if possible
Weeks 5-6 Add Weight ‚Ä¢ Hike 4-5 miles, 2 times per week
‚Ä¢ Increase pack weight to 20-25 lbs
‚Ä¢ Include elevation gain (stairs, hills, trails)
Weeks 7-8 Final Push ‚Ä¢ One LONG hike: 6-8 miles with full pack weight (30 lbs)
‚Ä¢ Test your boots, pack, and gear!
‚Ä¢ Week 8: Taper down, rest, and recover

üí° Training Tips for "Old Legs"

Listen to Your Body: Rest days are when your body gets stronger. Don't skip them!

Trekking Poles: Practice with poles during training. They reduce knee stress by 25%!

Break In Your Boots: Wear them on EVERY training hike. Your feet will thank you.

Strength Training: Add squats, lunges, and step-ups 2x/week. Strong legs = happy knees!

Stretch: 5-10 minutes of stretching after every hike prevents soreness.

‚ö†Ô∏è Don't Skip Training!

Why It Matters: Hiking 15 miles over 3 days with a 30-lb pack is hard if you're not prepared. Training makes it FUN instead of PAINFUL!

Injury Prevention: Weak muscles + heavy pack = injuries. Train to prevent blisters, knee pain, and back issues.

Enjoyment: The better your fitness, the more you'll enjoy the scenery instead of suffering!

üöê Shuttle Options

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical: This is a POINT-TO-POINT Trail!

You MUST arrange transportation! The trail starts at General Springs and ends at Blue Ridge Transfer Station - they're 20+ miles apart by road.

You need a vehicle at BOTH ends.

Option 1: Two-Car Shuttle (EASIEST!)

How It Works:

1. Drive two cars to the trip

2. Leave one car at Blue Ridge Transfer Station (END)

3. Everyone drives in the second car to General Springs (START)

4. Hike the trail

5. At the end, you have your car waiting at Blue Ridge Transfer Station!

6. Drive back to General Springs to pick up the first car

Pros: Free, simple, flexible timing

Cons: Requires two vehicles, 45-minute drive between trailheads

Option 2: Paid Shuttle Service

Arizona Trail Shuttles: Contact local outfitters in Payson for shuttle services

Typical Cost: $50-$100 per group (varies by service)

How It Works: They pick you up at the end and drive you back to your car at the start

Pros: Only need one car, someone else does the driving

Cons: Costs money, less flexible timing

Contact: Call Payson area outfitters or check the Arizona Trail Association for current shuttle providers

Option 3: Hitch a Ride (Not Recommended)

Hitchhiking is legal in Arizona BUT...

‚Ä¢ Traffic is VERY light on these forest roads

‚Ä¢ You might wait hours (or longer) for a ride

‚Ä¢ Safety concerns with hitchhiking

Verdict: Don't rely on this! Plan ahead with Option 1 or 2.

‚ö†Ô∏è Food Storage on the Trail

BLACK BEARS are present! You MUST hang your food every night.

How to Hang:

1. Use a bear bag or dry sack

2. Find a tree branch 15+ feet high, 6+ feet from the trunk

3. Throw rope over branch, hoist bag 12+ feet high

4. Tie off the rope to a separate tree

What to Hang: ALL food, toiletries, trash, anything scented!

When: Every night before bed, even if you're "just napping"

üêª Wildlife Safety

Black Bears

Present: Yes, black bears live in this area

Aggressive? No! Black bears are shy and avoid humans

Food Storage: HANG your food every night (see Food section above)

If You See a Bear:

‚Ä¢ Make noise, talk in a calm voice

‚Ä¢ Back away slowly, don't run

‚Ä¢ Make yourself look big (raise arms, jacket)

‚Ä¢ If attacked (rare!), fight back aggressively

Elk

Present: Very common! You'll likely see them

Dangerous? Only during rut season (Sept-Oct) when bulls are aggressive

What to Do: Keep 50+ feet away, don't approach, enjoy from a distance

Warning Signs: Bugling, antler displays, pawing ground = back off!

‚ö° Thunderstorms & Lightning

Risk: August/September afternoon thunderstorms are common!

Strategy: Start hiking early (6-7am), finish by early afternoon

If Caught in a Storm:

‚Ä¢ Get off ridges and high points immediately

‚Ä¢ Avoid isolated trees (lightning targets!)

‚Ä¢ Crouch low if you're in the open (feet together, head down)

‚Ä¢ Wait it out - storms usually pass in 30-60 minutes

üíß Hydration & Heat Illness

Condition Symptoms Treatment
Dehydration Dark urine, headache, fatigue, dizziness Drink water immediately, add electrolytes, rest in shade
Heat Exhaustion Heavy sweating, nausea, weakness, cool/clammy skin Stop hiking, rest in shade, drink water, cool with wet cloth
Heat Stroke No sweating, hot/dry skin, confusion, unconsciousness EMERGENCY! Call 911, cool person immediately, fan/wet them

‚ú® Prevention is Everything!

Hydrate: Drink BEFORE you're thirsty. Aim for 1 liter every 2 hours.

Electrolytes: Add electrolyte powder to your water. Sweat = salt loss!

Pace Yourself: Slow and steady wins the race. This isn't a race!

Rest Breaks: Take 10 minutes every hour. Snack, drink, recover.

Hike Early: Start at sunrise, finish by 2pm to avoid afternoon heat

üß≠ If Someone Gets Lost

STAY PUT! Don't wander - you'll get more lost.

S.T.O.P.: Sit, Think, Observe, Plan

Make Noise: Blow your whistle (3 blasts = distress signal)

Stay Visible: Find an open area, make an "X" with bright items

Stay Warm: Build a shelter, stay dry, conserve energy

Wait for Rescue: Search parties WILL find you if you stay put!
